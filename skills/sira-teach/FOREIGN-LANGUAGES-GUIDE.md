# Foreign Languages Guide

This is Sira's shareable foreign-languages guide. It distills language-learning practices into original guidance that can travel with the skill. Do not include private archives, child records, or long copied source passages.

## Mission

Foreign language study helps a child hear that the world can be named, sounded, sung, joked about, and understood in more than one way. It is not mainly vocabulary lists. It is a doorway into people, places, mouths, memory, culture, and humility.

The elementary aim is to:

- make foreign sounds and words physically familiar;
- adopt a language for a real family reason;
- learn words through images, actions, stories, songs, and use;
- connect language to places, people, food, travel, and culture;
- build old friends for sounds, words, phrases, songs, and scripts.

## Leading Words

- **Adopted language**: the language the family chooses to welcome for a real reason.
- **Mouth training**: hearing and producing unfamiliar sounds with the body.
- **Image word**: a word learned through picture, object, gesture, or context rather than translation alone.
- **Phrase friend**: a useful expression the child can actually say.
- **Culture bridge**: food, story, song, place, person, ritual, or trip that makes the language matter.

## K-12 Horizon

Elementary roots language in sound play, songs, names, greetings, phrases, images, stories, and joyful repetition.

Middle school can add grammar patterns, reading, conversation games, travel projects, pronunciation refinement, and larger vocabulary.

High school can add literature, film, debate, translation, identity, dialect, politics of language, and the irony that no translation is exact.

Do not rush the later tools. Let elementary foreign language be mouth, ear, image, song, and relationship.

## Pre-Elementary Ramp

Very young children can begin with sound and delight:

1. **Names**: family names, pet names, animal names in many languages.
2. **Songs**: repeated songs with gestures.
3. **Greetings**: hello, goodbye, thank you, yes, no.
4. **Objects**: five loved things in the room.
5. **Food and play**: words used while doing something.

The goal is not grammar. The goal is that another language feels possible and welcome.

## Elementary Threads

### 1. Taste Many, Adopt One

Let the family taste several languages through names, songs, foods, scripts, places, and sounds. Then adopt one for a real reason: family heritage, travel, neighbors, worship, friendship, books, music, or fascination.

### 2. Sound And IPA

Teach mouth positions, minimal pairs, and useful IPA symbols lightly. Sound differences are physical before they are intellectual.

### 3. Image-Based Words

Prefer pictures, objects, gestures, and scenes over English translation. A word should attach to an image or use.

### 4. Phrases And Play

Use small phrases in real family life: greetings, requests, games, table words, movement commands, praise, jokes.

### 5. Songs, Stories, Scripts

Songs and scripts make language magical. If the language uses a new script, treat it as a code and art form.

## Planning Cascade

### Year Aim

Adopt a language and build a small living base of sounds, words, songs, phrases, and cultural anchors.

### Season Focus

- language tasting;
- pronunciation and mouth training;
- family phrase set;
- image vocabulary;
- songs and gestures;
- script or alphabet;
- food and place;
- story or folktale.

### Week Rhythm

- one song or phrase routine;
- a few image words;
- one sound practice;
- one culture bridge;
- one old-friends check when due.

### Day Ritual

- greet in the language;
- point to one image word;
- sing one line;
- use one phrase at the table;
- hear one sound contrast;
- make or review one old friend.

## Doorways

- Minecraft: item names, place labels, commands, signs, treasure maps.
- Food: ingredients, menus, cooking verbs, market phrases.
- Animals: animal names, sounds, habitats, folktales.
- Music: songs, rhythm, pronunciation, cultural identity.
- Travel: maps, tickets, greetings, landmarks.
- Family: heritage, relatives, names, prayers, stories.
- Scripts: secret codes, calligraphy, inscriptions.

## Multiple Children

Share songs, phrase rituals, food, and culture bridges. Keep learner-specific evidence for pronunciation, confidence, reading, and vocabulary.

## Old Friends Examples

- **Sound**: "Which sound did you hear?"
- **Word image**: "Point to perro."
- **Phrase**: "Say thank you."
- **Song**: "Sing the next line."
- **Script**: "What sound does this letter make?"
- **Culture bridge**: "Where is this food from?"

## Resource Canon

Strong canon candidates:

- a real family reason for the language;
- songs and children's videos from native speakers, vetted by parent;
- picture dictionaries;
- Gabriel Wyner's `Fluent Forever` for parent support;
- Anki or Sira old-friends HTML for review;
- IPA resources;
- native-speaker friends, classes, library events, restaurants, cultural centers;
- maps, recipes, folktales, and music.

Substitutes:

- one song plus five phrases instead of a curriculum;
- parent-learning-alongside-child instead of expert instruction;
- image cards instead of translation lists;
- occasional cultural rituals instead of daily language blocks.

## Parent Interview Prompts

- Is there a family, travel, community, religious, or cultural reason for a language?
- Has the child shown interest in a language, script, song, food, place, or culture?
- Does the parent know the language, or will the family learn together?
- Is pronunciation, reading, conversation, heritage, or travel the main goal?
- How much daily sound practice is realistic?

## First Artifact Patterns

### Language Tasting

Name one loved animal, food, or person in several languages. Notice which sounds delight the child.

### Adopted Phrase Set

Choose five phrases the family can use this week.

### Mouth Mirror

Practice one unfamiliar sound with mirror, gesture, and silly words.

### Image Word Deck

Make five picture-first old friends with no English on the front.

### Song Loop

Choose one short song line with gesture and repeat all week.
