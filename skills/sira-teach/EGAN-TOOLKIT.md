# Egan Toolkit — Cognitive Tools, Planning Frameworks & the Learning Arc

The theory reference for `/sira-teach`. It distills Kieran Egan's *Imaginative Education* — chiefly **The Educated Mind** (1997, "TEM") for the theory and **An Imaginative Approach to Teaching** (2005, "IAT") for the planning frameworks — plus the **Lost Tools of Learning** practitioner archive (Brandon Hendrickson, losttools.substack.com) for how it plays out in a homeschool. Consult it whenever you design a lesson.

The governing idea (TEM): minds differ not by raw ability but by the **cognitive tools** a person has acquired. The tools are mostly linguistic, accumulate in layers as culture itself did (body → oral language → literacy → theory → reflexivity), and **never disappear** — earlier tools stay live and usable for life. So **teach the same truth through the tools the child actually holds**, not the tools you wish they had. A topic taught with the wrong toolkit fails not because it is "too hard" but because it is pitched to absent tools.

> **Stages are centers of gravity, not age-gates.** The five kinds of understanding overlap and layer; they do not replace one another. "Philosophic" tools (careful definitions) can be seeded early; "Somatic" understanding (the body, attachment, the ineffable) underlies everything. Read `LEARNER.md`, place the child by *evidence* of which tools are live, and reach freely up and down the stack.

## The five kinds of understanding & their cognitive tools

### Somatic (pre-language; ~birth–2, but lifelong)
Our original bodily way of making sense, beneath language for life. Tools: **bodily senses**; **emotional attunement** (feeling as orientation); **rhythm & musicality** (the prosody under language); **gesture**; **intentionality** (the body's purposive reach); a foundational **sense of a world** beyond words. Mostly pre-curricular, but the root of play, attachment, and the ineffable that art later chases.

### Mythic (~2–7/8; triggered by oral language)
The child organizes the world emotionally, through story and stark opposites. Tools (IAT ch.1):
- **Story** — the master tool. A story doesn't just carry information, it *orients emotion* toward content ("a musical score for the feelings"). Shape content into story form; this is not the same as inventing fiction.
- **Binary opposites** — the most basic organizing tool: emotionally charged pairs (good/bad, safe/scared, brave/cowardly, free/trapped, fair/unfair) give initial access to anything complex.
- **Metaphor** — seeing one thing as another; the root of inventiveness. Keep it vividly alive.
- **Rhyme, rhythm & pattern** — make any content memorable and attractive; central to learning every symbol system.
- **Jokes & humor** — expose how language works; keep the mind flexible.
- **Mental imagery from words** — images *generated from words* (not pictures shown) carry unique emotional force.
- **Gossip** — fitting events into narrative; the oldest verbal play.
- **Play** — frees behavior from objects; teaches norms and self-control.
- **Mystery** — a sense of the vast fascinating unknown beyond the everyday, drawing the mind into the adventure of learning.

### Romantic (~8–15; triggered by literacy)
The mythic tools "domesticated by literacy." The child reaches out to the real world through its extremes. Tools (IAT ch.2):
- **Extremes & limits of reality** — the *Guinness Book of Records* appetite: reality met first through its biggest, strangest, most terrible, most courageous edges.
- **Association with the heroic** — identifying with transcendent human qualities (courage, ingenuity, perseverance) to face a daunting world; lets you imbue any topic with importance.
- **Sense of wonder** — focusing on a thing until its particular astonishingness shows ("I wonder why…" is the start of science).
- **Collections & hobbies** — pouring intellectual energy into exhaustively mastering a bounded set.
- **Knowledge as human** — seeing knowledge as the product of real people's hopes, fears, and passions; *who* discovered it and what it cost them.
- **Narrative understanding** — narrative context establishes the emotional importance of knowledge (in physics no less than history).
- **Revolt & idealism** — resisting the adult world while wanting a place in it and wanting it better.
- **Changing the context** — escaping the sterile classroom by shifting the imaginative frame.
- **The literate eye** — facility with lists, flowcharts, diagrams, maps.

### Philosophic / Theoretic (~15–20; triggered by theoretic abstraction)
Building an abstract world of general ideas and connecting it back to the concrete. Tools (IAT ch.3): **sense of abstract reality** (making sense via general concepts); **sense of agency** (seeing oneself inside causal/historical networks); **grasp of general ideas & their anomalies** (build a scheme, hit its limits, rebuild it richer); **search for authority & truth** (assessing validity — the impulse behind encyclopedias); **meta-narrative understanding** (ordering facts into general ideas one is emotionally committed to).

### Ironic (~adult; triggered by reflexivity)
All the earlier tools, now held with: **reflexive awareness** (doubting one's own beliefs as one doubts others'); **awareness of language's/theory's limits**; **holding multiple meta-narratives at once**; **tolerance of contingency** (commitment and sympathy without secure foundations). The aim is not paralysis but the generous, flexible mind. Begins only embryonically in childhood — but you protect the path to it (see the philosophic framework's conclusion).

## The three planning frameworks

Egan replaces the standard "objectives → content → method → evaluation" template with **planning the lesson as a good story to be told**. The three frameworks share one shape — *find the emotional engagement → access it through the stage's tool → develop the body → conclude/mediate → evaluate* — retuned per stage. Pick by the child's live toolkit (`LEARNER.md`).

### A. Story-Form framework — Mythic learners
1. **Importance.** What is *emotionally* engaging about this topic? Why should it matter — not a "hook," but why it enlarges the child's world? (If *you* feel nothing in it, you won't engage the child — go back to the resources.)
2. **Binary opposites.** Which charged opposite best gives access? (Place value → *ingenuity vs. cluelessness*.)
3. **Organize into story form.** What content shows the opposites and shapes the topic into a *developing* story?
4. **Conclusion.** How to resolve the dramatic conflict in the opposites — and how far to *mediate* them?
5. **Evaluation.** How will you know it was understood, its importance felt, the content learned?

### B. Romantic framework — Romantic learners
1. **Heroic qualities.** What transcendent human quality sits at the heart of the topic, and what emotional images evoke it? (Industrial Revolution → *confident energy*; the chosen quality reshapes the whole unit.)
2. **Organize into a narrative**: **initial access** through content far from the everyday that embodies the quality / exposes an extreme of reality → **structure the body** along a clear narrative line → **humanize** the content (hopes, fears, intentions, wonder) → **pursue detail** (let the child go exhaustively deep on one aspect — the collections impulse).
3. **Conclusion.** Bring satisfying closure that still points onward and renews wonder.
4. **Evaluation.** Did it engage the imagination as well as convey content?

### C. Philosophic framework — Philosophic learners
1. **Powerful underlying idea.** What theory or meta-narrative best organizes the topic? (Look at what people *argue* about.)
2. **Organize into a theoretic structure**: make the theory *vivid* (initial access) → give a clear organizing meta-narrative (the body).
3. **Introduce anomalies** — start with *minor* ones, then gradually challenge the child's theory so they rebuild it more sophisticated.
4. **Present alternative theories** so they feel the limits of any one scheme.
5. **Encourage agency** — let them see themselves as actors inside these processes.
6. **Conclusion.** Crucially, do *not* destroy their theory — let them see it has a different status from fact, so losing certainty doesn't curdle into cynicism. (This is the bridge toward Ironic understanding.)
7. **Evaluation.** Did they build a general idea, elaborate it, and grasp its limits?

Once internalized, these become near-instant lenses — discard the explicit template.

> **The trivium, as a parallel appetite axis.** Alongside Egan's stages, Dorothy Sayers' *Lost Tools of Learning* names the child's appetite for a *kind of mental work*: **Grammar** (delight in memorizing, chanting, collecting — feed it facts, songs, rules), **Logic** (delight in argument, cause-and-effect, catching contradictions — invite debate, reward "but why?"), **Rhetoric** (delight in self-expression and making — have them synthesize, create, present). It roughly aligns with mythic→romantic→philosophic but is a distinct lens; track both in `LEARNER.md`.

## The learning arc (for a longer journey)

For a topic studied over many sessions (a Learning-in-Depth topic, a "Know a ___"), the Lost Tools practitioners wrap the whole journey in a four-phase narrative arc (after Gelmi / Cajete / Fettes). Use it to give a multi-session unit emotional continuity:

1. **Orient** — shake the ground; evoke wonder; give a reason to leave home. Tools: extremes, mysteries, heroes, an artistic spark.
2. **Complicate** — methodical study; refine the question; find and weigh sources. Tools: the scientific method, comparing sources, careful thinking.
3. **Transform** — accumulate real expertise; *make something original*; notice you've changed.
4. **Integrate** — closure that also opens; reflect on the journey; honor what stays unknowable. Tools: the beginnings of irony, the limits of knowledge.

## Wonder — the engine, in four dimensions

"Wonder" is not a superficial *wow*; it is a sustained state that anchors a whole journey. A topic can be made to wonder along four axes — reach for whichever fits:
- **Practical relevance** — knowing this lets you *do* something real.
- **Beauty** — the topic hides unexpected elegance.
- **Moral / existential weight** — it touches justice, meaning, fear, or who you are.
- **Mystery / paradox** — it reveals the world is stranger than assumed.

Metaphor, heroes, extreme facts, and withheld secrets are all *techniques of wonder* — ways to keep shifting how a topic is seen.

## Learning in Depth (LiD)

Egan's signature program (*Learning in Depth*, 2011): each child is **randomly assigned one topic** (apples, the wheel, dust, mollusks…) to study **for years**, building a personal portfolio to near-expert depth alongside the normal curriculum. Randomness (not "follow your passion") and a small assignment **ceremony** mark it as a lifelong relationship; the child soon outpaces the adult and grows autonomous. The payoff is *meta*: the child learns that **everything is fascinating if you learn enough about it**, meets the real texture of knowledge and inquiry, and gains humility before how much any one thing contains. At home this is the natural backbone for a "Know a ___" topic.

## Why this instead of conventional schooling (the short version)

Egan argues (TEM ch.1) that schools try to serve **three incompatible aims at once** — **socialization** (fit the child to society), **Plato's academic ideal** (privileged abstract knowledge), and **Rousseau's developmentalism** (unfold the child's nature) — and that pursuing any one undermines the others, which is why reform perpetually disappoints. His resolution is a single coherent aim: the cumulative **development of the kinds of understanding and their cognitive tools**. For a parent, the practical upshot is the permission this skill is built on — to teach *imaginatively and rigorously at the same time*, refusing the false choice between "fill their heads" (traditionalism) and "follow their passion" (progressivism). The third way: take the child's real hungers — story, mystery, justice, heroes, beauty, wonder — seriously, and feed them real content through them.

## Worked examples (from Egan)

- **Place value (Mythic).** Importance = the wonderful ingenuity of base-ten; opposite = *ingenuity vs. cluelessness*; told as the tale of a clever daughter who counts a king's vast army with ten councilors holding ten stones each — cascading tens into hundreds.
- **Industrial Revolution (Romantic).** Heroic quality = *confident energy*; initial access via the colossal chains of Brunel's *Great Eastern* (24,000 tons), forming an emotional bond with one man's audacity before the narrative widens.
- **French Revolution (Philosophic).** Organizing meta-narrative (e.g. "the Revolution as catastrophe") made vivid via Dickens's Madame Defarge knitting at the guillotine; then *anomalies* and *alternative* readings sophisticate the student's theory.
