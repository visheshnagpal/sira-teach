# Teaching Patterns — the reusable "how-to-teach" machinery

A catalog of portable techniques for making knowledge land and last, distilled from Brandon Hendrickson's **Lost Tools of Learning** archive (losttools.substack.com), itself built on Egan plus the cognitive science of memory. [EGAN-TOOLKIT.md](./EGAN-TOOLKIT.md) tells you *how to make a topic come alive*; this file tells you *how to make it stick and how to structure practice*. [USE-CASE-CATALOG.md](./USE-CASE-CATALOG.md) tells you *what to teach* per subject.

Reach for these when designing a lesson or a practice routine. Most cost almost nothing and run one-on-one at home better than they ever could in a classroom.

> **The one principle under all of them: lead with desire.** The honest lesson of the archive is that powerful retention tools (spaced repetition, deliberate practice) *fail without desire* — apathy, not forgetting, is what defeats learning, and these tools cannot be forced. So make a topic *matter* first (story, wonder, an emotional binary, a secret), then let the machinery below do its work *inside* that engagement. This is the same "engagement is the gateway" rule that governs the whole skill.

## Engagement & framing techniques

**Boss Questions.** Open a lesson with one posted problem that needs (almost) everything the lesson teaches, is *harder* than anything in it, and is a little ridiculous/captivating. The child tries it *before* being taught (never forced); if they crack it, they skip ahead; if not, the lesson becomes a *tool they now want*. Doubles as pre-assessment. Scales to a "Superboss" posted for a whole unit. Fails if made obligatory or graded.

**Riddle-Wrapped-in-a-Game.** Turn a lesson's core revelation into a riddle the child *thinks* they can answer but can't. They work to solve it; you feed stories, metaphors, vivid images, and little experiments as *hints*; a time limit ramps tension; reveal only if unsolved. Draw a "magic circle" (even turning chairs 90° signals different rules apply) so play feels licensed. A scalable substitute for the once-in-a-generation charismatic teacher.

**Secrets & Revelations.** Build the lesson around one mind-flipping fact — then *withhold* it. Treat it as a secret to be earned, so the payoff lands as a revelation rather than a told fact. (Scarcity makes it feel valuable; if the teacher hands it over freely, it must not matter.)

**Lying to Students.** Once in a while, fold a deliberate falsehood into the lesson and let the child try to catch it (you confirm). Trains skepticism with training wheels, raises attention, and sets up reveals — and produces the lovely twist where the child guesses a *true* astonishing thing is a lie, then is amazed it's real. Make it optional; warn the co-parent.

**Changing the context / the magic circle.** Escape the "we're doing school now" deadness by shifting the imaginative frame — the table is a spaceship, the week is a heist, the kitchen is a lab. Costs nothing, re-charges engagement.

## Retention & practice systems

**Forgetting Curve Box (Leitner box).** A physical card box with leveled dividers. Each day the child picks *one* fact they want to keep forever and writes it as a question/answer card. Quiz on a spreading schedule (right & easy -> advance a level; hard -> stay; wrong -> back to level 1). A nice variant maps the levels to natural rhythms (today / this week / Mon & Thu / monthly / by moon phase / by solstice), tying memory to the sky. Cap at ~1 card/day so it never becomes a chore. It doubles as *self-authorship* -- choosing what your future self will carry. For operational rules, read [MEMORY-BOX-GUIDE.md](./MEMORY-BOX-GUIDE.md).

**Spaced repetition, three ways.** The mechanism is "re-meet old friends before you forget them." Deploy it as (1) a **memory box** for fact-like knowledge; (2) **stretch one topic** over years so connection and review happen naturally (this is Learning in Depth / "Know a ___"); or (3) **spiral a specific beloved thing** — the same song, painting, story, or place revisited every couple of years, engaged through a higher toolkit each time (body → story → wonder → theory). The repeated object becomes a friend, not a flashcard.

**Concept Ladder.** For a finicky, foundational, interlocking concept (most of math, some grammar), build a two-column table: a simple question on the left, its answer on the right. Start *below* the child's level; add one tiny wrinkle per row; the last rows would have looked like sorcery minutes earlier. The child covers the answer column with a folded sheet, "climbs," and marks ✅/❌ to pinpoint exactly where understanding breaks. Re-attempt the ❌ rows daily until automatic. It is "Direct Instruction minus the teacher" — a guaranteed-understanding safety net. *Caution:* it works so well it tempts you to skip making the content alive — keep it a net, not the main event, and check the ladder for baked-in errors.

**Deep Practice Book.** A personal binder of hard problems the child *can't yet solve*. They recopy each problem (answer on the back), turn confusion into written questions, learn to *teach* the solution step-by-step, get help, and **re-solve every problem ~1–2×/week** even when it feels unnecessary — rating each attempt from "impossible" to "automatic." Converts enemies into tools and builds durable expertise. Requires real desire; it cannot be imposed.

**Cumulative Content.** Give *every* subject the respect math already gets: teach a small piece once, then deliberately *re-use* it so knowledge intertwines instead of decaying into trivia. Re-use is the antidote to forgetting. This gives the early years a clear mission — secure the "elements" that later learning will build on.

**Multiple Representations.** You cannot teach a general concept directly — abstraction emerges only when the brain connects many specific instances. So teach the most important, recurring ideas through *many superficially-dissimilar examples* and *openly* help the child name the shared pattern (the "aha"). The more important the concept, the more examples, and the more different they should be (e.g. "government" via a democracy, a dictatorship, and an anarchist collective).

**Art of Memory.** Teach and *routinely use* the historical memory arts — the memory palace (method of loci), the link system (absurd images for names), the major system (numbers) — to lock in genuinely useful sets (the constellations, classmates' names, a creed, vocabulary). "Weaponized foolishness": the sillier and more vivid the image, the better it holds. Pair with spaced repetition so it lasts.

## Reading, story & expression

**Book Tasting.** Once a month, pile up good books across genres; the child browses *covers only*, picks one, and you set a 5-minute timer to sample it, then sorts it into "keep" or "put back." Repeat. Matches the child to *their* books and teaches "reading selfishly" — the book serves you, not the reverse. The arbitrary restriction (cover-only, 5 minutes) is what makes it a game. Home version: the library, or "Book Christmas" at a bookstore.

**Living Books.** Steer toward books that "grab the brain" and carry the author's passion, and ditch the "dead" ones freely (Sturgeon's Law: most of everything is forgettable). A book is likely *alive* if it uses Egan's tools — emotional binaries, vivid images, wonder through bizarre detail, battling ideas, mystery. Love of reading is the master skill, so protect it.

**Story Ingredients.** From ~age 9 (earlier costs the naive joy of just *hearing* stories), have the child dissect narratives (a Pixar short works well) and learn a *few competing* recipes — never "the one way." A serviceable recipe: Character → Desire → Thwarted attempts (fail, fail bigger, last try) → Resolution → Change in the character (false belief → new belief). Turn it into a timed outline challenge. Teaches a real life skill *and* inoculates against manipulative stories by exposing how narrative works.

**The protégé effect.** Have the child *teach the idea back* — to you, a sibling, a stuffed animal. Teaching is the tightest retrieval practice there is, and it surfaces exactly what they don't yet understand.

## Using AI as a teaching aid (research accelerant, not tutor)

AI removes the historical bottleneck (a private library + years of reading) that made tutor-quality, imaginative content scarce. Use it to **speed-run your prep** so *you* can have the richer conversation — "fewer screens, richer stories, deeper questions." Egan's tools are what you use to prompt it well.

Concrete moves:
- **Eganize a dull topic on demand:** ask it to teach long division / photosynthesis / comma rules through a story grounded in a character's challenge and an **emotional binary a young child can grasp** — and to *list the real historical details first* for accuracy.
- **Generate scaffolding:** memory-palace options, story packets (image + 4-paragraph story + discussion questions + pronunciation), counting rhymes, IPA breakdowns.
- **"Eganize-an-LLM" prompt:** keep a reusable prompt that forces personification, metaphor, and emotional oomph rather than dry abstraction (see the "Plant MatchMaker" archetype in the catalog).

Cautions — taken seriously:
- **Always ask "what's the warranty here?"** AI helps *certain* learners with *certain* thinking (it gravitates to single-right-answer, algorithmic tasks). It is weak on big ideas that are "true and false at once."
- **The "infinitely patient tutor" is an *ineffective* one.** Good teachers are *infinitely impatient* — they don't wait to be asked; they invite, cajole, compel. Most children never ask for help. Be the proactive presence the machine can't be.
- **It hallucinates.** Verify ("make sure these events really happened"). Use it as research accelerant, never as the source of truth — that is what `RESOURCES.md` is for.

## Honest caveats (where this is hard)

- **Desire is the missing piece.** The most powerful tools here demand a child who *wants* to use them, and want cannot be forced — forced practice is going-through-the-motions with near-zero benefit. Egan's imaginative tools are "the door that opens" toward desire; treat them as prerequisite, not optional polish.
- **The best engagement techniques are fragile to obligation.** Boss Questions, Lying, Riddles, ungraded ciphers all *die* the moment they become mandatory, graded, or policed. Protect their playfulness.
- **Concept Ladders can be too seductive.** Their reliability tempts you to stop making content alive. Keep them a safety net.
- **"Alive" and "wonderful" are partly subjective**, and dissecting stories costs some of the naive joy of hearing them — which is why some techniques wait until the child is older.
- **It looks "unserious" to outsiders.** Done well, hard ideas arrive wrapped in play, story, and fun, so it is easily misread as floofy. The rebuttal is the rigor underneath: real experiments uncovered, real terms introduced (as "frozen metaphors") after the metaphor, real depth reached.
