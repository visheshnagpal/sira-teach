# Vocabulary And Writing Guide

This is Sira's shareable vocabulary and writing guide. It distills word, sentence, speaking, and composition practices into original guidance that can travel with the skill. Do not include private archives, child records, or long copied source passages.

## Mission

Vocabulary and writing help a child love words, notice sentences, shape thoughts, tell stories, persuade, remember, and become more real to themselves. This subject is not mainly spelling lists or five-paragraph forms. It is the craft of saying something worth saying.

The elementary aim is to:

- grow a loved, usable store of words;
- imitate strong sentences and speech patterns;
- write or dictate real thoughts, stories, jokes, descriptions, and arguments;
- connect writing to attention, memory, and voice;
- build old friends for words, phrases, sentence patterns, poems, and speeches.

## Leading Words

- **Word treasure**: a word worth keeping because it is funny, exact, beautiful, useful, or strange.
- **Sentence pattern**: a reusable shape for thought.
- **Voice**: the child's way of sounding like themselves while becoming more capable.
- **Imitation**: copying a strong pattern to make it one's own.
- **Audience**: the person the words are trying to reach.

## K-12 Horizon

Elementary roots writing in oral language, copywork, dictation, imitation, journaling, narration, jokes, letters, lists, poems, and playful sentences.

Middle school can add structure: paragraphs, arguments, story craft, revision, grammar as a tool, rhetoric, public speaking, and audience.

High school can add essays, style, satire, research, criticism, speeches, dialectic, and the irony that language can reveal, manipulate, and conceal.

Do not rush the later tools. Let elementary writing begin with speech, delight, imitation, and real reasons to write.

## Pre-Elementary Ramp

Very young children enter through speaking, drawing, dictation, and word play:

1. **Say it**: child tells; parent writes.
2. **Name it**: exact words for textures, sounds, feelings, motion.
3. **Play it**: rhymes, jokes, silly sentences, alliteration.
4. **Draw and caption**: one picture, one dictated sentence.
5. **Treasure words**: choose one loved word from a story or day.

The goal is not handwriting stamina. The goal is that words feel powerful and personal.

## Elementary Threads

### 1. Loved Words

Collect words from books, comics, songs, family life, nature, games, and history stories. Prefer words found in rich context over random lists.

### 2. Strong Sentences

Give sentence patterns to imitate before naming grammar. Let the content be silly or vivid so the structure becomes visible.

### 3. Narration And Retelling

After a story, event, experiment, or walk, ask the child to tell what happened. Shape it gently: first, then, but, so, finally.

### 4. Personal Writing

Use tiny, real forms: one-sentence diary, thank-you note, field note, caption, joke, sign, recipe, speech, complaint, invitation, memory card.

### 5. Public Voice

Practice very short speeches, recitations, jokes, and teach-backs. The body and voice are part of writing.

## Planning Cascade

### Year Aim

Build a confident word-and-sentence life: the child gathers words, imitates patterns, dictates or writes real thoughts, and revises without shame.

### Season Focus

- word treasure and vocabulary;
- copywork and sentence imitation;
- oral narration;
- jokes and humor;
- personal diary or field notes;
- story writing;
- short speeches;
- letters and real audience writing.

### Week Rhythm

- one word treasure;
- one sentence imitation;
- one oral narration or dictation;
- one tiny real writing act;
- one old-friends check when due.

### Day Ritual

- choose one word;
- copy one sentence;
- dictate one thought;
- tell one thing that happened;
- make one joke better;
- read one line aloud.

## Doorways

- Minecraft: signs, guidebooks, quest logs, base descriptions, creature reports.
- Animals: field notes, creature descriptions, poems, care instructions.
- Food: recipes, reviews, menus, sensory words.
- Sports: commentary, player bios, rules, persuasive arguments.
- Stories: alternate endings, character letters, retellings.
- Family: letters, captions, family sayings, remembered stories.

## Multiple Children

Share read-alouds, word treasure, jokes, and speeches. Keep writing output learner-specific because stamina, spelling, handwriting, and voice differ widely.

## Old Friends Examples

- **Word**: "What does vivid mean?"
- **Phrase**: "Use 'on the other hand' in a sentence."
- **Pattern**: "Start a sentence with an action: 'Cougars prowl...'"
- **Poem line**: "Say the next line."
- **Speech move**: "Look up before the last sentence."
- **Spelling**: "Write because."

## Resource Canon

Strong canon candidates:

- loved picture books, comics, poems, songs, and read-alouds;
- a family word notebook;
- `The Art of Styling Sentences` for parent support;
- joke books and riddle books;
- Matthew Dicks's story practice for parent inspiration;
- poetry anthologies;
- dictionaries with etymologies;
- speech timer or simple recording tool.

Substitutes:

- oral dictation instead of handwriting;
- captions instead of paragraphs;
- comic panels instead of essays;
- family text messages or letters as real audience writing.

## Parent Interview Prompts

- Does your child like talking, jokes, stories, facts, songs, comics, or quiet drawing?
- Can they write by hand comfortably? For how long?
- Do they resist spelling, handwriting, composing, or reading aloud?
- What books, shows, games, or songs are supplying language right now?
- Is the goal expression, mechanics, confidence, public speaking, or school readiness?

## First Artifact Patterns

### Word Treasure Card

Choose one loved word from context. Say it, act it, draw it, use it, and save it.

### Sentence Imitation

Offer one strong sentence pattern and three playful substitutions.

### One-Sentence Diary

At day's end, write or dictate one sentence future-self will be glad to have.

### Joke Lab

Tell a joke, ask why it works, change one part, perform it.

### Tiny Speech

Prepare a 30-second talk about a loved thing. Practice stance, voice, and one clear ending.
