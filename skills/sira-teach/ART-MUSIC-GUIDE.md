# Art And Music Guide

This is Sira's shareable art-and-music guide. It distills visual art, music, drawing, sound, and family-canon practices into original guidance that can travel with the skill. Do not include private archives, child records, or long copied source passages.

## Mission

Art and music help a child attend, feel, make, hear, see, imitate, judge, and return. They are not mainly enrichment. They are ways of knowing: a painting, song, film, rhythm, doodle, or sound can carry perception and emotion that prose cannot.

The elementary aim is to:

- build repeated encounters with great and beloved works;
- help the child see, hear, mimic, move, draw, sing, and make;
- create a family canon of songs, images, movies, and sounds;
- connect art and music to history, geography, story, science, and memory;
- return to works across years with deeper tools.

## Leading Words

- **Encounter**: a live meeting with a work, not a lecture about it.
- **Canon**: works the family keeps in play.
- **Mimicry**: using the body, voice, hand, or instrument to understand.
- **Mood**: the felt quality a work carries.
- **Spiral return**: revisiting a work later through richer attention.

## K-12 Horizon

Elementary roots art and music in repeated looking, listening, moving, singing, drawing, making, and story.

Middle school can add artists, periods, instruments, genres, technique, critique, parody, cultural context, and comparison.

High school can add aesthetics, politics of canon, interpretation, modernism, sacred art, propaganda, taste, and the irony that art can be both deeply personal and culturally made.

Do not rush the later tools. Let elementary art and music be encounter, imitation, mood, and making.

## Pre-Elementary Ramp

Very young children enter through body and repetition:

1. **Listen again**: one song many times.
2. **Move**: march, sway, clap, stomp, freeze.
3. **Look**: name colors, shapes, faces, animals, action.
4. **Mimic**: pose like a figure in a painting.
5. **Make marks**: doodle, scribble, draw sound, copy shapes.

The goal is not technique. The goal is that art and music become ordinary parts of family attention.

## Elementary Threads

### 1. A Song A Week

Return to one song across the week: listen, move, draw the mood, notice instruments, learn a line, tell the artist's story, make a parody or mnemonic.

### 2. A Painting Or Image A Week

Look closely before explaining. Guess what is happening. Mimic poses. Imagine sounds, smells, temperature, and the moment before and after.

Three named methods make this concrete, and you can mix them:

- **Visual Thinking Strategies (VTS)**: ask just two questions, over and over — "What's going on here?" and "What do you see that makes you think that?" Simple enough to use on any image, a plate of food, or an aquarium.
- **Charlotte Mason's "Picture Study"**: choose one artist and a few works; say a little about the artist's life; look silently for a few minutes; cover the image and have the child describe it from memory; then look again together for what was missed, and go deeper. Built for long-term return.
- **"Touching the Art"** (Luc Travers): a step-by-step way to find and live inside the story elements of a work — best for art with human or human-like subjects.

### 3. Mnemonic Songs

Distinct from "A Song a Week": use geeky songs whose job is to lodge hard details — states and capitals, formulas, the bones, spelling rules — in memory. Read the lyrics, talk about what they mean, act them out, and sing them while marching or doing dishes. Outdated science lyrics are a feature, not a bug: they show how ideas change. This thread is about remembering, where "A Song a Week" is about feeling and thinking. (Backing: `Geeky Songs`.)

### 4. Drawing And Making

Draw often and lightly. Begin with doodles, copying, cartoons, many versions, and "draw this twelve ways." Do not rush realism. The aim is hundreds of happy drawings, not correctness. Fancy "draw what you really see" methods are wonderful for a child who already loves drawing, but can kill it for one who does not — lead with fun.

### 5. Making Noise

Explore sound with body, objects, instruments, materials, rooms, echoes, rhythm, and silence. Tap, rub, thwack, and blow on ordinary things and listen to what they answer. Make mouth sounds — whistles, pops, clicks, honks — before reaching for "real" instruments, which are just well-engineered noise-makers. (Backing: `Making Noise`.)

### 6. Strange Sensations

Treat the body's senses as a way of knowing, since art began as an attempt to share what words cannot. Put the child in touch with textures, smells, and tastes; take away sight (a blindfold, a mystery box) so one sense can lead; make it a guessing game, and have them put the experience into a few careful words. Watch for allergies and sensory sensitivities. (Backing: `Strange Sensations`.)

### 7. Drawing Sound And Mysterious Music

Sit with powerful music where words are small or absent — film scores, chant, flamenco, Indian classical, drumming, gamelan, and the songs of birds and whales. Then "draw the sound": pick a color and a shape beforehand, listen once, and let a non-representational drawing carry the feeling (no pictures of objects, no words). Compare drawings and notice how differently each person heard it. (Backing: `Mysterious Music and Drawing Sound`.)

### 8. Art Of Memory

Memory itself can be an art. Teach the few techniques people have carried for centuries — the method of location (a "memory palace"), the link system (join dull facts to vivid, absurd images), and the major system for numbers — and actually use them to hold things worth keeping. They work because the brain treats places and images as special, and rewards a little incongruity, emotion, and humor. (Backing: `Art of Memory`.)

### 9. Film And Moving Image

Use real films and shorts, not only educational media. Watch for story, mood, music, image, and moral imagination.

## Planning Cascade

### Year Aim

Build a family canon and a habit of making: songs, images, doodles, rhythms, films, and return rituals.

### Season Focus

- songs and rhythm;
- paintings and close looking;
- drawing practice;
- sound and instruments;
- sensory art (textures, smells, tastes);
- mysterious music and drawing sound;
- films and visual story;
- art from one culture or era;
- family canon building;
- art connected to a history or geography arc.

### Week Rhythm

- one featured song, image, or film scene;
- one mimicry or movement activity;
- one making task;
- one story or context note;
- one old-friends check when due.

### Day Ritual

- listen to one song;
- look at one image for one minute;
- draw one object;
- clap one rhythm;
- ask what mood the work carries;
- save one line, image, or sound as an old friend.

## Doorways

- Minecraft: builds, architecture, texture, soundscapes, maps, pixel art.
- Movies: soundtrack, scene composition, character themes.
- Animals: animal art, calls, movement, masks.
- Sports: chants, logos, uniforms, stadium songs.
- Food: still life, table rituals, festival music.
- History: period art, propaganda, songs of movements.
- Family: songs, wedding music, religious art, heirloom images.

## Multiple Children

Shared encounters work well across ages. Keep learner-specific evidence for attention, sensitivity, making, confidence, and critique.

Use two success cues: younger child points, moves, mimics, or chooses; older child compares, interprets, names technique, or explains context.

## Old Friends Examples

- **Work**: "Which painting is this?"
- **Artist**: "Who painted this?"
- **Song line**: "Sing the next line."
- **Mood**: "Is this calm, tense, playful, or solemn?"
- **Instrument**: "What instrument do you hear?"
- **Technique**: "Where is the light coming from?"

## Resource Canon

Strong canon candidates:

- a family playlist;
- museum websites and public-domain art collections;
- printed art cards or a rotating wall image;
- child-friendly instruments and noise makers;
- drawing books for kids: Ed Emberley's step-by-step books and Lee J. Ames's `Draw 50...` series (also Randall Munroe's stick-figure work for older kids);
- Mona Brookes's drawing method, which builds from a few elements — dots, circles, straight lines, curved lines, angles (her published program is "Monart" — *editor-added* identifier);
- Betty Edwards, `Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain` — "draw what you see" practice, best held for children who already love drawing;
- Fred Newman, `MouthSounds` — a sound-effect artist's guide for the Making Noise thread;
- close-looking methods: Visual Thinking Strategies, Charlotte Mason's "Picture Study," and Luc Travers's `Touching the Art` (touchingtheart.com);
- mnemonic-song makers such as Schoolhouse Rock, They Might Be Giants, and Animaniacs, for the memory thread;
- high-quality films and shorts, vetted for age.

Substitutes:

- one song repeated all week instead of lessons;
- museum images online instead of field trips;
- household objects for instruments;
- doodle notebook instead of formal art curriculum.

## Parent Interview Prompts

- What songs, films, images, colors, instruments, or making activities already attract your child?
- Does your child resist drawing, singing, dancing, or being watched?
- What art or music matters to the family culture?
- Are there noise, screen, religious, or content sensitivities?
- Do you have instruments, art supplies, museum access, or a printer?

## First Artifact Patterns

### One Song Station

Choose a song. Listen, move, draw the mood, learn one line, and save one old friend.

### One Painting Close Look

Look first. Ask the two VTS questions — "What's going on here?" and "What do you see that makes you think that?" Pose like one figure and imagine what they hear, smell, and feel. Then reveal title and context. (For a longer version, run the Picture Study steps: look silently, cover the image, describe from memory, look again for what was missed.)

### Draw Twelve Ways

Pick one ordinary object and draw it many ways without judging.

### Sound Hunt

Find five household sounds and compare material, pitch, volume, and mood.

### Family Canon Card

Add one work to the family canon with why it matters.
