# Geography Guide

This is Sira's shareable geography guide. It distills geography practices into original guidance that can travel with the skill. Do not include private archives, child records, or long copied source passages.

## Mission

Geography helps a child know that the world is real, vast, varied, placed, and worth loving. It is not mainly map labeling. It is the child's first great connector: stories, animals, weather, food, languages, history, science, art, travel, and family life all happen somewhere.

The elementary aim is to plant the world in the child's head and body:

- know the big pieces of the planet;
- attach vivid marvels to real places;
- orient in actual space;
- connect local place to the wider world;
- build old friends that keep places alive over time.

## Leading Words

- **Map**: a picture that matches a place from above.
- **Marvel**: a real place that makes the world feel larger.
- **Navigation**: knowing where you are, where things are, and how to find your way.
- **Local place**: the home ground that makes far places feel real by analogy.

## K-12 Horizon

Elementary roots the child in names, places, directions, marvels, local geography, and a felt sense that maps describe reality.

Middle school can complicate this with climate, biomes, trade, migration, terrain, borders, energy, cities, and how geography shapes history.

High school can add geopolitical reasoning, cartographic distortion, environmental systems, human geography, economics, colonialism, conflict, and the irony that every map reveals and hides at the same time.

Do not rush the later tools. Let elementary geography be concrete, mythic, sensory, playful, and memorable.

## Pre-Elementary Ramp

If a very young child loves maps, begin. Do not wait for formal grade readiness.

Young children may treat maps as ordinary pictures. De-abstract maps through role-play:

1. **Body**: point to front, back, left, right, near, far, above, below.
2. **Room**: pretend to be a bird above the room. Draw what the bird sees.
3. **Playground or yard**: pretend to be a hawk. Draw paths, trees, doors, swings, corners.
4. **Home or building**: pretend to float above the whole building.
5. **Neighborhood**: imagine looking down from an airplane.
6. **Country**: imagine looking from a rocket.
7. **World**: imagine looking from the Moon.

For preschool and kindergarten, the goal is not accurate cartography. The goal is that the child begins to feel that a map corresponds to a place.

## Elementary Threads

### 1. Maps

Start with the biggest things: continents and oceans. Add one new place at a time (a place a day is a comfortable pace), review old friends, and linger if the child is hungry for rivers, mountains, countries, or regions.

Then quietly aim for completion. The big payoff comes from a child who actually holds the whole world in their head, so set the horizon high and walk toward it over years:

1. continents and oceans;
2. the major countries (going by population is a fair, fight-free way to pick which to learn first);
3. the major cities and the most striking features (highest mountains, driest deserts, biggest rivers, richest reefs and rainforests);
4. then fill in whole sets: all the countries of a continent, all the states or provinces of your nation, and eventually all 195 UN-recognized nations.

It does not matter much where the "major countries" line falls, precisely because the destination is completion. Front-load what feels most important so a child who stops partway still has the most useful pieces.

Make maps meaningful by using a story shape:

- **Orient**: start with an above-view role-play or a question that makes the map matter.
- **Complicate**: move from room to playground to neighborhood to town to country to world.
- **Transform**: create old-friends cards for continents, oceans, and major features.
- **Integrate**: ask what the child now wonders about the world.

Google Earth is arguably the greatest map ever made, and it is a core tool for this thread: zoom from your own roof out to the Pacific, drop into any place a story mentions, and scout local features. But the best map is still the one you can point to in two seconds without opening an app, so keep a real wall map up alongside it.

### 2. Marvels

Marvels are vivid real places: painted deserts, caves, islands, enormous walls, ancient cities, strange lakes, sacred mountains, reefs, meteor craters, hidden churches, wild animal migrations.

Use marvels to make places emotionally sticky:

1. Choose one marvel.
2. Find it on a map or globe.
3. Read or tell a short account.
4. Look at one excellent image.
5. Imagine the place through senses: sound, smell, temperature, texture, scale.
6. Make a memory card with the marvel name, what it is, and where it is.
7. Keep a master list of marvels and point to them on a map weekly.

Start with a local marvel when possible. Then ask what marvels the whole world might hold.

### 3. Navigation

Navigation makes geography real instead of bookish.

Start inside the home:

- Put north, east, south, and west cards on the right walls.
- Use directions casually: "the book is on the east shelf."
- Use color and image anchors: cold north, sunrise east, sunset west, warm/tropical south. Adapt for hemisphere and local reality.

Then play "glass walls":

- Imagine the walls disappeared.
- Point toward the kitchen, street, playground, library, ocean, capital city, a chosen marvel.
- Let the child notice when the flat-world imagination breaks. Save globe complications for later unless the child asks.

Then go outdoors:

- Track the Sun's path across the day.
- Notice where shadows point.
- Learn a few local sky anchors or constellations when appropriate.
- Notice nature clues such as slope, wetness, wind, plant growth, and shade, while warning that no single clue is reliable.

Navigation should train attention. The child should become more awake to the actual world.

## Planning Cascade

### Year Aim

Give the child a living map of the world: continents, oceans, local place, directions, and a growing set of marvels.

### Season Focus

Pick one dominant thread for 6-10 weeks:

- maps and big features;
- marvels of the world;
- local geography;
- directions and navigation;
- sky and orientation.

### Week Rhythm

A useful week might include:

- one new map feature or marvel;
- one old-friends review;
- one map/globe pointing game;
- a short, frequent map drill (Seterra works well; even a few minutes most days adds up fast toward completion);
- one local noticing walk or direction practice;
- one short reference or memory artifact.

### Day Ritual

A useful day can be tiny:

- point to one place;
- tell one short story;
- draw one above-view map;
- ask one question;
- make or review one old-friends card;
- notice one direction outdoors.

## Doorways

Use the child's current fascination as the doorway.

- Minecraft: maps, coordinates, biomes, rivers, mountains, routes, bases, scale, resource locations.
- Animals: migration, habitats, islands, oceans, deserts, forests.
- Food: where ingredients grow, trade routes, climates, markets.
- Sports: countries, stadiums, travel, flags, time zones.
- Stories: where the story happens, what the land makes possible, journeys.
- Family: ancestral places, moves, trips, languages, weather, landscapes.

The doorway opens geography; it does not replace geography.

## Multiple Children

Use the same geography subject folder for the family, but keep learner evidence separate when children differ.

- Shared: `geography/MISSION.md`, `ARC.md`, `RESOURCES.md`, broad `GLOSSARY.md`, maps, books, and family routines.
- Individual: learner profile, current doorway, learning records, old-friends cards, and live edge.

When two children work together, choose a shared ritual with two success cues: an easier embodied cue for the younger child and a richer explanation, map move, or memory challenge for the older child.

## Old Friends Examples

Use mixed card types:

- **Map image**: "Which continent is this?"
- **Place to map**: "Point to the Pacific Ocean."
- **Marvel**: "Where is the Great Barrier Reef?"
- **Direction**: "Which wall is north?"
- **Gesture**: "Show east with your body."
- **Route**: "How would we get from home to the library?"
- **Local clue**: "Where does the Sun rise from our breakfast window?"

Keep reviews quick and joyful. Reschedule missed reviews without drama.

## Resource Canon

Use family-owned and library-accessible resources first. Good geography resources are visual, revisitable, and specific.

Strong canon candidates:

- A globe.
- A large wall map.
- A local street or trail map.
- A child-friendly world atlas.
- Google Earth as a core, free map tool: zoom from home to ocean, fly to any place a story names, scout local features.
- Seterra (seterra.com): a free map-quiz tool that drills continents, oceans, countries, capitals, states, and features by tap-to-find. It is the workhorse for the drive toward completion, and it has built-in custom maps (for example, the most populous nations). A gentle warning: it is mildly addictive.
- `Atlas Obscura Explorer's Guide for the World's Most Adventurous Kid`.
- `Atlas Obscura: An Explorer's Guide to the World's Hidden Wonders`.
- `Atlas Obscura Wildlife: An Explorer's Guide to the World's Living Wonders`.
- H. A. Rey's `Find the Constellations` for sky navigation.
- H. A. Rey's `The Stars: A New Way to See Them` for adult support.
- Tristan Gooley's `The Natural Navigator` for adult support in nature navigation.
- Gillian Judson's `A Walking Curriculum` for local sensory walks.

Substitutes:

- library atlas instead of owned atlas;
- printed outline maps instead of purchased map sets;
- Seterra or printed blank maps instead of paid quiz workbooks;
- public-domain or museum images instead of paid books;
- a neighborhood walk instead of a field trip;
- a phone compass only as a check, not as the main teacher.

## Parent Interview Prompts

Ask one at a time:

- What places does your child already talk about?
- Do they like maps, globes, flags, animals, weather, buildings, travel, Minecraft, food, or stories?
- Can they understand "above" and "looking down"?
- Do they know any continents, oceans, countries, states, or local landmarks?
- What local places matter to your family?
- Do you have a globe, wall map, atlas, or local map?
- How much daily geography energy is realistic: 2 minutes, 10 minutes, or a weekly block?

## First Artifact Patterns

### Bird Map

For pre-elementary or early elementary: draw the room from above, as if seen by a bird. Then place a toy on the real floor and mark it on the paper.

### One Big Thing

Introduce one continent or ocean. Use a globe, image, gesture, and one memory card.

### Local Marvel

Choose a nearby place that feels special. Visit or remember it, then ask what marvels the whole world may contain.

### Direction Cards

Put direction cards on the walls. Use them casually for one week.

### Marvel Card

Read one marvel, find it, imagine being there, and make a card: name, what it is, where it is.

### Ten-Nation Drill

Together, guess the ten most populous countries, then check on a map. Then open Seterra's matching map and try to tap them all. Miss some? Run it again. This is the smallest taste of the drive toward completion, and it is the easy on-ramp to learning more countries over time.
