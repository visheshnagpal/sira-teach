# Philosophy And World Religions Guide

This is Sira's shareable philosophy-and-world-religions guide. It distills big-question, virtue, proverb, religion, and law practices into original guidance that can travel with the skill. Do not include private archives, child records, or long copied source passages.

## Mission

Philosophy and world religions help a child ask what is real, good, beautiful, sacred, fair, knowable, and worth doing. They are not mainly a separate unit. They run through stories, history, science, art, family life, conflict, death, celebration, and law.

The elementary aim is to:

- honor real child questions without forcing abstraction;
- give the child stories, proverbs, rituals, virtues, and thought games to think with;
- introduce world religions through stories, art, practices, places, and questions;
- connect moral and metaphysical questions to lived examples;
- build old friends for proverbs, virtues, stories, questions, and terms.

Religions are worth teaching for several plain reasons: you cannot understand the world (or many of the people in it) without them; they hold strange and useful insight; and a child who never learns them is more easily fooled by whichever version reaches them first online. The aim is literacy and good judgment, not conversion.

## Leading Words

- **Big question**: a question that keeps opening rather than closing.
- **Thought game**: a playful situation that makes an abstract issue concrete.
- **Virtue**: a named strength the family wants to recognize and practice.
- **Worldview**: a way people understand reality, value, and human life.
- **Sacred literacy**: knowing religious stories, art, practices, and ideas well enough to understand the world.

## K-12 Horizon

Elementary roots philosophy and religion in stories, questions, proverbs, virtues, rituals, places, and thought games.

Middle school can add arguments, comparisons, debates, world religions, justice, law, identity, and competing moral frameworks.

High school can add metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, theology, atheism, ideology, pluralism, critique, and the irony that deep traditions can be both wisdom-bearing and dangerous.

Do not rush the later tools. Let elementary work be concrete, story-rich, respectful, and question-honoring.

## Pre-Elementary Ramp

Very young children already ask philosophy. Three simple games can be played with children as young as three. They come from the Philosophy with Children movement (founded by Carlo Maria Cirino, now led by Alessandro Gelmi), and they prepare a child for the seminar-style discussion that comes later in the early grades:

1. **Word chain**: one person says a word; the next says a related word, and so on. When a step is strange, stop and ask how the two connect.
2. **One object**: put down a spoon, sock, or pine cone and ask what it is like. Then push the edges: could a spoon be made of air? Could you use it on the Moon? Is a cup a kind of spoon?
3. **Strange situations**: state an odd premise and ask how it could have happened. A dog is found on Mars; a bluebird weighs as much as an elephant.
4. **Fairness moments**: real disputes over sharing, rules, promises.
5. **Sacred stories and rituals**: family traditions, holidays, songs, images.

The goal is not debate. The goal is to honor wonder and make thinking playful.

## Elementary Threads

### 1. Philosophy Everywhere

Pause when a real subject produces a big question. Do not manufacture abstractions constantly. Catch living questions when they arise.

### 2. Proverbs And Microliterature

Use proverbs as tiny thought objects: a proverb is the most experience packed into the fewest words. Offer a few each week, picked to be diverse in meaning and origin. Copy each into a special spot (this doubles as handwriting practice), restate it in your own words, argue over what it means, commit it to memory, and connect it to stories the child already knows. Highlight disagreements rather than settling them.

### 3. Named Virtues

Choose a handful of family virtues, rather than trying to honor every good quality at once. Pursue them through stories, heroes, sacrifices, failures, and real family decisions. Avoid stories that end in a tidy "moral"; good stories carry their values inside the action, not in a closing sentence. Let the child also question and test the virtues, not just obey them.

### 4. World Religions Everywhere

Include religious stories, art, buildings, festivals, practices, music, ethics, and questions across history, geography, literature, art, and family life. The subjects already teem with religious material; the work is mostly to stop keeping it out. A concrete elementary practice is to learn the short numbered doctrines by heart, the way many traditions do with scripture: the Ten Commandments, the Four Noble Truths, the Five Pillars. The aim is sacred literacy and respect, not adopting or refuting any one faith.

### 5. Law And Justice

Use strange real cases and child-scale disputes to practice reasons, sides, rules, consequences, and fairness. Tell the story that led to the case, name the questions it raises, assign sides, argue it, declare a winner, and only then reveal how it really turned out. For older children, "The Case of the Speluncean Explorers" (a famous fictional case from a 1940s law review article, freely readable online) is a rich one, because every judge in it reaches a different verdict for a different reason.

## Planning Cascade

### Year Aim

Build a small set of living questions, virtues, proverbs, sacred stories, and justice cases that the child can return to.

### Season Focus

- preschool thought games;
- family virtues;
- world proverbs;
- one religious tradition through stories and art;
- holidays and rituals;
- fairness and law;
- big questions from science or history;
- sacred places and houses of worship.

### Week Rhythm

- one proverb, story, case, or thought game;
- one connection to another subject;
- one real-life noticing or family practice;
- one old-friends check when due.

### Day Ritual

- ask one big question;
- restate one proverb;
- notice one virtue in action;
- compare two answers;
- tell one sacred or moral story;
- ask what would be fair.

## Doorways

The doorway is the child's current obsession — stories, animals, space and science, food, family life, law, a favorite show or game, Minecraft, whatever it is right now. Start wherever the child already burns hot.

- Stories: moral choices, promises, identity, courage, death, sacrifice.
- Animals: minds, personhood, care, suffering.
- Space/science: reality, mystery, origins, knowledge.
- Food: ritual, taboo, hospitality, festivals.
- Family: values, holidays, prayer, grief, gratitude, rules.
- Law: arguments, sides, evidence, fairness.
- Minecraft: rules, ownership, justice, worlds, creation, griefing, cooperation.

## Multiple Children

Shared stories and questions work well, but do not force one child's depth on another. The younger child can answer through examples and stories; the older child can compare reasons, traditions, and implications.

## Old Friends Examples

- **Proverb**: "What does this proverb mean?"
- **Virtue**: "Name one act of courage."
- **Question**: "Can something be fair and still hurt?"
- **Story**: "Which tradition tells this story?"
- **Term**: "What is a ritual?"
- **Case**: "What was the rule in this case?"

## Resource Canon

Strong canon candidates:

- family stories, traditions, rituals, and values;
- proverb collections from many cultures: Jon R. Stone, `The Routledge Book of World Proverbs`, and the searchable site worldofproverbs.com (searchable by topic or culture; vet for whether attributions are genuine);
- children's books about world religions, vetted for respect and accuracy;
- local houses of worship or cultural centers, visited respectfully;
- Hershovitz, `Nasty, Brutish, and Short` for parent support;
- Stephen Prothero and Huston Smith for parent world-religion support;
- strange legal cases for older children;
- art, music, architecture, and festivals.

Substitutes:

- one proverb a week instead of a curriculum;
- family virtue stories instead of abstract lessons;
- museum or video tours when visits are not possible;
- child-scale justice cases before legal texts.

## Parent Interview Prompts

- What religious, philosophical, or ethical traditions does the family want honored?
- Are there traditions or materials to avoid or handle carefully?
- What big questions has the child asked recently?
- Which virtues matter most to your family?
- Is the goal literacy, family formation, open inquiry, apologetics, pluralism, or moral practice?
- What local communities, rituals, holidays, or sacred places are accessible?

## First Artifact Patterns

### One Object Question

Pick one object and ask what could change before it stops being itself.

### Proverb Of The Day

Read, restate, argue with, and memorize one proverb.

### Virtue Spotting

Name one family virtue and find it in a story, history event, or day.

### Sacred Image Look

Look carefully at one religious artwork or building image and ask what it is trying to show.

### Tiny Trial

Use a child-scale dispute to argue both sides before deciding.
