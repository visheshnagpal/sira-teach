# Memory Box Guide

Use this when creating old-friends cards, designing a review rhythm, or deciding whether spaced repetition belongs in the next artifact.

## Core Idea

Spaced repetition in Sira is not a drill add-on. It is a way for the child to choose what their future self should keep meeting.

Call it **old friends** with the family. Use technical terms like spaced repetition, Leitner, interval, and ease only in internal files or when the parent asks.

## Three Forms

1. **Memory box**: for fact-like knowledge, names, places, vocabulary, procedures, poems, chants, map images, and marvels.
2. **Long acquaintance**: one topic stretched over months or years, such as Know a Tree, Know a Place, Know an Animal, or a long-running geography marvel collection.
3. **Spiral return**: one beloved work, place, story, song, image, ritual, or question revisited across ages with richer tools of understanding.

Choose the form before making cards. Not everything deserves a card.

## What Becomes A Card

Prefer cards that came from desire:

- the child chose it as wonderful, funny, strange, useful, beautiful, or worth keeping;
- it anchors a real subject arc;
- it came from a loved book, place, song, route, experiment, story, or doorway;
- it unlocks future practice;
- it is a marvel that helps a place, person, number, word, or process become memorable.

Avoid cards made only because an adult thinks the fact is important. If the fact is important but lifeless, first make it alive through story, image, rivalry, wonder, gesture, use, or a real problem.

## Small Daily Ritual

For preschool, kindergarten, first grade, and early elementary, begin lightly:

1. At the end of a day or lesson, ask: "What is one thing from today you want future-you to remember?"
2. Make at most one new card unless the child is begging for more.
3. At the start of the next day or session, re-meet 1-3 due old friends.
4. Stop before it becomes a chore.

For a child who cannot write yet, the parent writes the card. The child may draw, point, act, sing, choose the image, or dictate.

## Review Signals

Use the same four signals everywhere:

- **Again**: missed or resisted; meet again soon.
- **Hard**: correct with effort or help; keep interval short.
- **Good**: secure enough; lengthen interval.
- **Easy**: effortless and bright; lengthen more, or retire if it has become trivial.

These are scheduling signals, not grades.

## Friendly Schedules

Use a simple digital due date when needed. For a physical box, use dividers such as:

- later today;
- tomorrow morning;
- Monday and Thursday;
- Friday;
- first and final quarter moon;
- full moon;
- spring and fall equinox;
- winter solstice.

Plain calendar labels are fine. Sky-rhythm labels are useful when they make memory feel tied to the real world.

## Multiple Children

When more than one child is learning:

- shared family old friends may live in a shared subject deck;
- each child still needs separate review evidence;
- siblings may review together, but do not let one child's fluency hide another child's uncertainty;
- if a card is meaningful for only one child, keep it in that child's learner deck.

## When To Update State

A single review result updates the card schedule, not the child's durable profile.

Update `LEARNER.md`, `MISSION.md`, `ARC.md`, or `RESOURCES.md` only from accrued signal: repeated review patterns, repeated delight, repeated resistance, a parent correction, or evidence that a card belongs in a different arc.
