# Mathematics Guide

This is Sira's shareable mathematics guide. It distills mathematics practices into original guidance that can travel with the skill. Do not include private archives, child records, or long copied source passages.

## Mission

Mathematics helps a child see pattern, quantity, shape, change, proof, and structure in the world. It is not mainly worksheet completion. It is a language of reality and a craft of thinking: the child learns to count, compare, measure, build, estimate, puzzle, argue, and notice invariants.

The elementary aim is to make numbers and shapes feel usable and alive:

- build secure number sense and operation sense;
- connect symbols to bodies, objects, stories, games, and real measurements;
- practice enough for fluency without deadening curiosity;
- develop courage around hard problems;
- build old friends for facts, procedures, images, and strategies that deserve return.

## Leading Words

- **Quantity**: how many, how much, how long, how heavy, how likely, or how often.
- **Model**: an object, drawing, gesture, story, or diagram that makes an abstract idea thinkable.
- **Ladder**: a carefully sequenced path from easy certainty to one new wrinkle.
- **Puzzle**: a problem that invites effort because the child wants the reveal.
- **Quixotic goal**: an ambitious, slightly ridiculous math feat that gives practice romance.

## K-12 Horizon

Elementary roots mathematics in quantity, pattern, operations, shapes, measurement, games, and delight. The child should feel that numbers are tools, not punishments.

Middle school can turn numbers against reality: Fermi estimates, ratios, probability, Bayes, algebra, proof beginnings, scale, and the heroic need for precision.

High school can add abstraction, functions, geometry, statistics, modeling, calculus, infinity, proof, philosophy of math, and the irony that mathematics is both invented and discovered.

Do not rush the later tools. Let elementary math be embodied, playful, accurate, and cumulative.

## Pre-Elementary Ramp

Very young children enter mathematics through body, rhythm, sorting, and everyday quantity:

1. **Body**: fingers, steps, jumps, claps, left/right, near/far, more/less.
2. **Objects**: count snacks, blocks, stairs, shoes, spoons, coins.
3. **Patterns**: clap patterns, bead patterns, color patterns, movement patterns.
4. **Comparison**: bigger/smaller, heavier/lighter, longer/shorter, full/empty.
5. **Stories**: "three bears", "one more dragon", "two went away".

For preschool and kindergarten, the goal is not formal arithmetic. The goal is that number and shape become familiar companions.

## Elementary Threads

### 1. Number Sense

Make numbers physical before they become symbolic. Use fingers, beads, ten frames, blocks, dice, cards, number lines, money, cooking, and measurement.

Keep asking: what does this symbol point to in the world?

### 2. Operations And Fluency

Addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division should grow from actions: joining, separating, grouping, sharing, scaling, and repeated structure.

Use old friends for math facts and procedures, but do not reduce math to recall. Fluency supports thinking; it is not the whole subject.

### 3. Concept Ladders

When a foundation is fragile, build a ladder: one tiny step per row, beginning below the child's level and ending just beyond it. The child should be able to see exactly where understanding breaks.

Use ladders for place value, regrouping, fractions, negative numbers, exponents, algebra moves, and unit conversion.

### 4. Origin Stories

Every symbol and procedure in math was invented by a real, often quirky person solving a real problem. Tell the short story behind a squiggle as you teach it: why "+" is a shorthand for the Latin "et" (and), why "3" is three lines swooped together, why we call them "integers." A sentence or two is enough. This turns a page of dead symbols into history congealed, and lets a child feel they are joining a centuries-long human tradition rather than memorizing arbitrary marks. Use this lightly and often; never force a child to memorize the names.

### 5. Deep Practice Book

Too much hard-won math is learned, then forgotten, including the very problems a child struggled with most, which are the ones worth keeping. Build a collection of the problems that have stumped the child. Give each its own page, with room for questions and more than one solution method. Return to them on a schedule (often at first, then more rarely), and each time ask the child to rate it: impossible, hard, medium, easy, or automatic. Did you notice anything new? Is there a simpler way? Over months and years, a child watches "impossible" problems drift to "easy," which is its own quiet proof that present frustration is temporary.

### 6. Puzzles And Games

Puzzles keep effort alive. Use ciphers, pattern games, strategy games, estimation bets, mental math challenges, magic tricks, and classics like Tower of Hanoi.

### 7. Measurement And Reality

Measurement connects math to care. Cook, build, weigh, time, walk, estimate, compare, and check. Teach units through felt anchors before formulas.

## Planning Cascade

### Year Aim

Build one secure layer of mathematical power: number sense, operations, measurement, shapes, or fractions, plus a living puzzle thread.

### Season Focus

Pick one dominant focus for 6-10 weeks:

- counting and place value;
- addition and subtraction;
- multiplication and division;
- fractions through sharing and measuring;
- shapes and spatial reasoning;
- measurement and estimation;
- puzzle season;
- mental math or abacus season.

### Week Rhythm

A useful week might include:

- two short practice sessions;
- one concept ladder or hard-problem conversation;
- one puzzle or game;
- one real measurement or making task;
- one old-friends check when due.

### Day Ritual

A useful day can be tiny:

- count something real;
- solve one ladder row;
- estimate then measure;
- play one dice or card round;
- explain one strategy aloud;
- make or review one old friend.

## Doorways

The doorway is the child's current obsession — cooking, money, sports, building, games, art, machines, a favorite show or book, Minecraft, whatever it is right now. Start wherever the child already burns hot.

- Cooking: fractions, ratios, time, temperature, scaling recipes.
- Money: counting, place value, decimals, budgeting, tradeoffs.
- Sports: scores, rates, probability, records, geometry.
- Building: measurement, symmetry, angles, load, area, volume.
- Games: strategy, probability, combinatorics, logic.
- Art: symmetry, pattern, tiling, proportion.
- Minecraft: coordinates, scale, area, volume, resources, ratios, redstone logic.

The doorway opens mathematics; it does not replace mathematics.

## Multiple Children

Use a shared math folder for family rhythms and resources, but keep learner evidence separate. Math levels can diverge sharply.

Shared games, puzzles, and real-world projects can carry two success cues: an embodied or counting cue for the younger child, and a symbolic, strategic, or explanatory cue for the older child.

## Old Friends Examples

- **Fact**: "What is 7 x 8?"
- **Procedure**: "Show regrouping for 42 - 18."
- **Image**: "Draw three fourths."
- **Strategy**: "How can 9 + 6 become easier?"
- **Unit anchor**: "About how long is one meter?"
- **Puzzle move**: "What is the legal move in Tower of Hanoi?"

Review math old friends quickly. If review pressure grows, split a separate review ritual from the living lesson.

## Resource Canon

Strong canon candidates:

- manipulatives: beads, blocks, dice, cards, measuring tape, scale, clock, coins;
- JUMP Math (John Mighton) as a source for carefully sequenced, ladder-style practice;
- alternative core curricula families love: Beast Academy, Minimalist Math (Michelle Scharfe, of ResearchParent.com), and Math Academy. Pick whichever fits the child; the practices in this guide work on top of any of them;
- a Deep Practice Book: a plain notebook or binder kept by the child for stumper problems (see First Artifact Patterns);
- The Complete Book of Finger Math by Edwin Lieberthal, the guide behind the finger-as-abacus method (out of print but cheap used and on Kindle);
- a real soroban or abacus;
- Prime Climb and other number-rich games;
- Arthur Benjamin's mental math work for parent inspiration;
- 3Blue1Brown for older-child or parent visual intuition;
- Ben Orlin for mathematical humor and adult support;
- cooking, shopping, building, maps, and sports statistics as real sources.

Substitutes:

- homemade counters instead of purchased manipulatives;
- hand-drawn ladders instead of workbooks;
- household measurement instead of lab equipment;
- oral mental math instead of screen practice.

## Parent Interview Prompts

Ask one at a time:

- What math can your child already do easily?
- Where do they freeze, guess, or resist?
- Do they like numbers, puzzles, building, money, cooking, games, maps, or Minecraft?
- What curriculum, manipulatives, or games do you already own?
- How much daily math energy is realistic?
- Is the goal confidence, fluency, catch-up, enrichment, or practical use?

## First Artifact Patterns

### Number Treasure

Pick one number. Build it with fingers, objects, dice, equations, a story, and a drawing.

### Concept Ladder

Make a tiny ladder for one fragile move. Stop at the first real wobble.

### Boss Problem

Offer one hard, interesting problem first, then teach the tool needed to solve it.

### Deep Practice Book

Start a notebook. The next time a problem genuinely stumps the child, give it a full page. Re-solve it together a few days later and rate it together: impossible, hard, medium, easy, automatic.

### Measurement Hunt

Estimate and measure five things in the room. Make one unit anchor old friend.

### Puzzle Ritual

Play one short strategy or number game, then ask the child to explain one move.
