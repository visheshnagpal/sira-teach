# Literature Guide

This is Sira's shareable literature guide. It distills reading, story, poem, and family-canon practices into original guidance that can travel with the skill. Do not include private archives, child records, or long copied source passages.

## Mission

Literature helps a child live inside stories, language, images, characters, worlds, and questions larger than their own life. It is not mainly comprehension questions. It is a way to furnish the imagination and build a family culture of books, poems, myths, jokes, songs, and sayings.

The elementary aim is to:

- protect love of reading and being read to;
- build a family canon of living books and stories;
- retell, enact, quote, memorize, and play inside stories;
- notice characters, desires, conflicts, settings, and changes;
- return to beloved works across years with deeper tools.

## Leading Words

- **Living book**: a book that carries life, voice, images, and real human attention.
- **Family canon**: works the family keeps returning to and thinking with.
- **Retelling**: making a story one's own by telling it again.
- **Character desire**: what someone wants strongly enough to move the story.
- **Spiral return**: revisiting a beloved work later with richer understanding.

## K-12 Horizon

Elementary literature roots the child in read-alouds, fairy tales, myths, poems, picture books, jokes, biographies, and stories worth retelling.

Middle school can add genre, character arcs, competing interpretations, author craft, historical context, and story structure.

High school can add criticism, irony, canon debates, literary theory, sacred texts as literature, translation, tragedy, satire, and the question of what stories do to us.

Do not rush the later tools. Let elementary literature be love, voice, memory, play, and repeated encounter.

## Pre-Elementary Ramp

Very young children enter through lap reading, rhythm, repetition, pictures, and play:

1. **Read again**: repetition is not a flaw; it is how stories become friends.
2. **Chant and rhyme**: poems, nursery rhymes, songs, refrains.
3. **Point and wonder**: pictures before analysis.
4. **Act it**: voices, gestures, stuffed animals, blocks.
5. **Choose**: let the child sort books into more, later, and no.

The goal is not early literary analysis. The goal is attachment to words and stories.

## Elementary Threads

### 1. Read-Aloud Life

Read aloud beyond the child's independent reading level. Protect pleasure. Stop or pause books that are dead for this child.

### 2. Book Tasting

Let the child sample many books briefly and selfishly. Covers, first pages, pictures, and curiosity matter.

### 3. Playing Inside Stories

Return to a story through acting, drawing, Lego, song, alternate endings, changed settings, new points of view, props, maps, and character desire.

### 4. Memory And Recitation

Memorize poems, lines, jokes, speeches, prayers, or passages that are worth carrying. Use old friends lightly.

### 5. Family Canon

Keep works in play: quote them, compare new life to them, revisit them seasonally, and let them become shared language.

## Planning Cascade

### Year Aim

Build a living shelf of beloved works and practices: read aloud, retell, memorize, play, and choose.

### Season Focus

- fairy tales and folktales;
- myths and legends;
- poetry and rhyme;
- picture-book study;
- chapter read-aloud;
- jokes and humor;
- biographies as stories;
- one author or series.

### Week Rhythm

- daily or near-daily read-aloud;
- one retelling or play move;
- one poem, joke, or line;
- one book-tasting or library move;
- one old-friends check when due.

### Day Ritual

- read one scene;
- ask what someone wants;
- quote one line;
- act one moment;
- draw one image;
- choose the next book.

## Doorways

- Minecraft: quest logs, world lore, maps, survival stories, myths of a place.
- Animals: animal tales, field guides as literature, fables.
- Humor: joke books, comic strips, funny poems.
- Heroes: myths, biographies, epics.
- Art: picture books, illustrated classics, wordless books.
- Family: stories from childhood, cultural tales, repeated sayings.

## Multiple Children

Read-alouds and family canon can be shared. Independent reading, sensitivity, attention, and retelling demands should stay learner-specific.

Use two success cues: younger child acts, points, repeats, or chooses; older child explains desire, change, genre, or perspective.

## Old Friends Examples

- **Line**: "Say the next line."
- **Character**: "What does Anansi want?"
- **Story order**: "What happens after the door opens?"
- **Image**: "Which story has this picture?"
- **Genre**: "Is this a trickster tale or a quest?"
- **Poem**: "Recite the first stanza."

## Resource Canon

Strong canon candidates:

- library card and regular library ritual;
- fairy tale and folktale collections;
- mythology collections;
- poetry anthologies for children;
- excellent picture books;
- comic strips and graphic novels, vetted for fit;
- audiobooks;
- family and cultural stories;
- Visual Thinking Strategies for picture reading.

Substitutes:

- audiobooks instead of parent read-aloud when tired;
- oral family tales instead of printed books;
- picture walks instead of full reading;
- one poem memorized slowly instead of many poems skimmed.

## Parent Interview Prompts

- What books or stories does your child ask for again?
- Do they like funny, scary, heroic, cozy, factual, animal, magical, or realistic stories?
- What do they reject?
- How sensitive are they to fear, death, cruelty, embarrassment, or suspense?
- Can they read independently, or is this mostly read-aloud?
- What languages and cultural story traditions belong in the family?

## First Artifact Patterns

### Book Tasting

Gather five books. Sample each for five minutes. Sort into now, later, and no.

### Play Inside One Scene

Act a scene with objects, then ask what each character wanted.

### Line Worth Keeping

Choose one beautiful or funny line. Say it, copy or dictate it, and make an old friend.

### Story Map

Draw the places in a story and move a token through them.

### Spiral Return Note

Mark one beloved work to revisit next season or next year.
