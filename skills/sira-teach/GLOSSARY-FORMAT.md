# GLOSSARY.md Format

`GLOSSARY.md` is the child's living language for a subject. It can hold words, names, symbols, phrases, gestures, distinctions, chants, images, and meanings the child can actually use. Building it is part of learning.

## Structure

```md
# {Subject} Glossary — {child or family}

{One sentence on what this glossary covers.}

## Words we know

**Seed**:
A tiny sleeping plant with its own packed lunch, waiting for water and warmth to wake up.
_We say "seed", not_: pip, kernel

**Sprout**:
The first little green shoot a seed sends up when it wakes.
_We say "sprout", not_: shoot, seedling

## Symbols, gestures, and images

**North arrow**:
{What it means, how the child uses or recognizes it}

## Phrases and chants

**{Phrase}**:
{When we say it, what it helps us remember}
```

## Rules

- **Add a term only when the child can use it correctly.** The glossary records compressed understanding — it is not a dictionary the child reads to learn. Wait for evidence (they used it right, unprompted).
- **Meanings in the child's register.** Tight, concrete, and vivid — built from images and metaphors the child holds, not textbook phrasing. One or two sentences is usually enough.
- **Be opinionated about which word.** When several words mean the same thing, pick one and list the rest as "we say X, not…". This is how a child's language compresses and firms up.
- **Reuse the glossary's own words inside definitions.** Once a word is in, prefer it everywhere — including inside other definitions. This is what makes harder words easier later.
- **Group under subheadings** when natural clusters appear. A flat list is fine when terms cohere.
- **Revise as understanding deepens.** A week-one definition may be wrong by week six. Update in place; don't leave stale entries. Capturing a *better* definition is itself a sign of learning — consider a learning record.
