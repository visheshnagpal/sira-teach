# Sciences Guide

This is Sira's shareable sciences guide. It distills science practices into original guidance that can travel with the skill. Do not include private archives, child records, or long copied source passages.

## Mission

Science helps a child notice that reality is stranger, deeper, and more law-governed than it first appears. It is not mainly vocabulary lists or demonstrations that pretend to explain. It is disciplined wonder: observe, compare, question, test, model, name, and revise.

The elementary aim is to make the child awake to the real world:

- observe living and nonliving things closely;
- collect vivid mysteries and weird truths;
- connect terms to real mechanisms;
- practice simple experiments and careful noticing;
- build long acquaintance with local nature, sky, body, matter, and machines.

## Leading Words

- **Wonder**: a real feature of the world that feels worth explaining.
- **Mechanism**: what is actually happening underneath the surface.
- **Model**: a metaphor, diagram, experiment, or simulation that helps but is not the thing itself.
- **Mystery**: a known unknown, not a failure of teaching.
- **Specimen**: one concrete thing studied closely over time.

## K-12 Horizon

Elementary science roots the child in observation, weird facts, local nature, sky, body, matter, and simple causal models.

Middle school can add systems, invisible mechanisms, experiments, data, classification, energy, evolution, chemistry, and Earth systems.

High school can add mathematical modeling, uncertainty, scientific revolutions, competing theories, ethics, and the irony that models both reveal and distort reality.

Do not rush the later tools. Let elementary science be sensory, strange, concrete, and alive.

## Pre-Elementary Ramp

Very young children enter science through senses and repeated noticing:

1. **Touch and compare**: smooth, rough, sticky, cold, warm, heavy, light.
2. **Watch living things**: plants, ants, worms, birds, pets, mold, seeds.
3. **Weather and sky**: clouds, rain, shadows, Sun, Moon, stars.
4. **Cause and effect**: roll, drop, float, sink, mix, melt, freeze.
5. **Name carefully**: not to recite terms, but to see more.

For preschool and kindergarten, the goal is not explanation mastery. The goal is reverent attention.

## Elementary Threads

### 1. Local Nature

Know a tree, bird, insect, plant, rock, stream, garden bed, or weather pattern. Return over seasons. Name it, draw it, measure it, tell its story, and notice changes.

### 2. Weird Mechanisms

Use surprising truths to puncture false simplicity: flowers are reproductive structures, planets fall around the Sun, birds are living dinosaurs, the Moon appears in daytime, plants sense and respond.

### 3. Experiments That Explain

Avoid experiments that only perform spectacle. Ask what the experiment actually shows, what it does not show, and what model it uses.

### 4. Scale And Systems

Move between tiny, human, Earth, and cosmic scale with images, gestures, maps, and models. Use scale as wonder, not as dead numbers.

### 5. Science Stories

Tell the human story of discoveries, mistakes, instruments, rival theories, and stubborn evidence. Science is history congealed.

## Planning Cascade

### Year Aim

Build a habit of close observation plus a small cabinet of genuine mysteries, local specimens, and true mechanisms.

### Season Focus

- know a tree or local organism;
- sky and shadows;
- body and senses;
- plants and growth;
- animals and classification;
- matter, mixing, and materials;
- machines and forces;
- one science-is-weird theme.

### Week Rhythm

- one observation;
- one story or weird truth;
- one drawing, measurement, or comparison;
- one experiment or model;
- one old-friends check when due.

### Day Ritual

- notice one change outdoors;
- ask "what is really happening?";
- draw one specimen;
- compare two things;
- make a prediction;
- name one mystery.

## Doorways

- Minecraft: biomes, mining, materials, circuits, ecology, gravity mods.
- Animals: classification, behavior, habitats, evolution, anatomy.
- Space: scale, gravity, phases, light, planets, stars.
- Cooking: chemistry, heat, phase change, fermentation.
- Machines: forces, energy, gears, levers, electricity.
- Gardening: plants, soil, insects, weather, seasons.
- Body: senses, bones, muscles, sleep, digestion.

## Multiple Children

Share specimens, walks, experiments, and wonder stories. Keep learner-specific evidence: the younger child may point, sort, imitate, or notice; the older child may explain, predict, model, or challenge.

## Old Friends Examples

- **Specimen**: "Which tree are we knowing this year?"
- **Mechanism**: "What is a flower for?"
- **Sky**: "Where did the Sun rise today?"
- **Classification**: "Why is a bird a dinosaur?"
- **Model caution**: "What does this experiment not show?"
- **Vocabulary**: "What does evaporation mean?"

## Resource Canon

Strong canon candidates:

- a nature notebook;
- magnifying glass, jar, thermometer, ruler, scale;
- Merlin Bird ID and local field guides;
- H. A. Rey sky books;
- `Science is Weird` as inspiration where available;
- David George Haskell, Daniel Chamovitz, Stefano Mancuso, Tristan Gooley for parent support;
- museum, aquarium, planetarium, garden, park, beach, forest, or local nature center;
- reputable public science videos and museum resources, vetted for age.

Substitutes:

- one backyard plant instead of a field trip;
- phone photos instead of microscopes;
- library field guides instead of purchased books;
- kitchen experiments instead of kits.

## Parent Interview Prompts

- What living things, machines, body questions, space topics, or weird facts already fascinate your child?
- What local nature is easy to revisit?
- Do you have outdoor access, pets, plants, tools, or science kits?
- Is your child sensitive to body, death, predators, disasters, or gross topics?
- How much mess and outdoor time is realistic?

## First Artifact Patterns

### Know One Thing

Choose one local specimen and begin a long acquaintance: name, draw, touch, measure, revisit.

### What Is Wrong With This Picture?

Show a common simplified model and ask what is misleading about it.

### Weird Truth Card

Introduce one true strange fact, connect it to a real mechanism, and make one old friend.

### Prediction Walk

Predict what will change outdoors, then check.

### Model And Warning

Use a simple model, then name what the model cannot show.
